Questions to Ask Before Hiring a Residential Contractor

By the Editorial Team | Published: July 2026 | Last Updated: July 2026

Why the Interview Matters More Than the Estimate

Most construction disputes trace back to decisions made before a contract was ever signed. A homeowner who asks the right questions during the interview stage filters out the majority of future problems: unlicensed operators, underinsured crews, chronic schedule-slippers, and firms whose pricing looks attractive only because the scope is vague.

The questions below apply to any residential contractor - remodeler, general contractor, or full-service builder. They are organized by what each answer actually reveals, because the goal is not to collect paperwork but to understand how a company behaves under pressure.

Are you licensed for this type of work in this state?

Licensing requirements vary by state and by project type, and the correct license matters - a home improvement registration is not the same as a construction supervisor license where both exist. Ask for the license number and verify it directly with the state licensing board rather than accepting a photocopy.

What insurance do you carry, and can your agent send certificates directly?

A contractor should carry general liability insurance and workers' compensation for every person on site. The critical detail is requesting the certificate of insurance from the contractor's agent, not the contractor - certificates handed over personally can be expired or altered. Uninsured workers injured on a property can create liability for the homeowner.

Who actually holds the license - you, or someone else?

Some operations "borrow" a license from an affiliated individual who never visits the site. The license holder should be materially involved in supervising the work.

Can I speak with three past clients, including one from several years ago?

Recent references reveal how a firm communicates today; older references reveal how the work has held up. Ask past clients specific questions: Did the project finish on budget? How were disagreements handled? Would you hire them again without hesitation?

Can I visit a job currently in progress?

An active site tells the truth. Look for organized material storage, protected finishes, posted permits, and crews wearing appropriate safety equipment. A contractor reluctant to show a live site is answering the question by declining it.

How many projects like mine have you completed?

Volume in a different specialty does not transfer cleanly. A firm that excels at kitchen remodels may be poorly equipped to manage a ground-up build with sitework, structural engineering, and a year-long schedule.

What exactly does this price include - and exclude?

Low bids frequently win by omission. Ask for the exclusions list in writing, and ask how allowances were set. An allowance is a placeholder dollar amount for an unselected item, such as flooring or fixtures; unrealistically low allowances make a bid look cheaper than the finished project will ever be.

How do you handle change orders?

The answer should describe a written process with pricing approved before work proceeds. "We'll work it out as we go" is how budget overruns are born.

What is the payment schedule?

Payments should track completed milestones, not the calendar. Be cautious of any request for a large upfront deposit beyond what materials procurement reasonably requires; several states cap initial deposits by law.

Who will be on my site every day, and who is my single point of contact?

The persuasive person in the sales meeting is often not the person running the job. Ask to meet the project manager or lead carpenter who will actually manage daily work.

None of these flags requires interpretation. Each one, on its own, is sufficient reason to keep looking.

Verification Beyond the Interview

Answers can be checked, and the diligent half hour it takes is worth more than any impression from a meeting. Court record searches in the contractor's operating counties surface litigation patterns - one dispute means little, a recurring cast of unpaid suppliers means everything. The local building department can confirm whether the contractor pulls permits routinely and passes inspections without drama; inspectors know every builder in town, and some will say so. Supplier references - the lumberyard, the plumbing house - reveal whether accounts are paid on time, which is the most reliable proxy for financial health a homeowner can access.

Online reviews reward careful reading rather than star-count skimming. Look for how the contractor responds to criticism, whether complaints cluster around the same failure (schedule, cleanup, communication), and whether the five-star reviews describe projects like yours or one-day repairs.

From General Vetting to Choosing a Builder

These questions apply to any residential contractor, but hiring a firm to design and build an entire home adds another layer of evaluation - design capability, estimating transparency, and long-horizon project management. That deeper process is a dedicated evaluation process for design-build custom home builders.

How many contractors should I interview?

Three is the practical standard. Fewer than three gives no basis for comparison; more than five usually produces diminishing returns and scheduling fatigue on both sides.

Should I always take the lowest bid?

No. Bids are only comparable when scope, allowances, and exclusions match. A lower number with thinner scope is not a lower price - it is a deferred one.

What should be in the written contract?

Scope of work, total price, allowances, payment schedule tied to milestones, start and substantial-completion dates, change-order procedure, warranty terms, and dispute resolution. If it was promised verbally, it belongs in the document.

How do I verify a contractor's license?

Every state that licenses contractors maintains a public lookup through its licensing board or consumer affairs office. Search by license number and confirm the name, status, and expiration date match the person you are hiring.

What if a contractor is offended by these questions?

Established professionals answer them routinely and often volunteer the information first. Defensiveness in the interview stage is a preview of communication during construction.

How far ahead should I start interviewing contractors?

Three to six months before the intended start for mid-size projects, and longer for major builds - good firms book out seasons in advance, and a contractor who can mobilize a full crew tomorrow is either unusually lucky or unusually unbooked, which is worth asking about. Early conversations also let the contractor contribute pricing input while the design can still respond to it.

Should the contractor visit my property before bidding?

Yes, always. A bid produced without seeing site access, existing conditions, and utility locations is a guess with a signature. The site visit is also diagnostic in reverse: a contractor who measures, photographs, and asks questions during the walkthrough is showing you how they will run the job.
