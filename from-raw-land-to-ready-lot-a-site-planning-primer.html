From Raw Land to Ready Lot: A Site Planning Primer

By the Editorial Team | Published: July 2026 | Last Updated: July 2026

The Lot Is the First Design Decision

Before a house has a floor plan, it has a piece of ground - and the ground votes first. Its slope decides foundations and driveways, its soils decide wastewater and drainage, and its regulatory overlay decides where anything may stand at all. Homeowners who investigate these constraints before purchase buy the lot they think they are buying; those who investigate afterward discover what they actually bought. This primer walks the four investigations that convert raw land into a ready, buildable site; the full sequencing discipline behind them is developed in Site Development and Whole-Home Planning for Custom Homes.

Investigation One: The Terrain - Topographic Survey

A topographic survey maps the third dimension a deed ignores: contours, slopes, drainage paths, ledge, trees, wetland flags, and existing features, referenced to a datum every subsequent professional shares. Slope is money - a house placed along the contours on the gentlest workable ground minimizes excavation and retaining structure, while a placement fighting the grade pays in machine hours and concrete. On any parcel with meaningful relief, the survey is the cheapest insurance in the project and the document design cannot responsibly begin without.

Investigation Two: The Soils - Perc Test and Wastewater

Where municipal sewer does not reach, the lot must treat its own wastewater, and whether the soil can do that job is measured, not assumed. The percolation test times how fast water drains at leach-field depth; the deep observation hole exposes the soil profile, ledge, and seasonal high groundwater. The results size the septic system, cap the bedroom count, and - because codes require a reserve field area plus setback rings from wells, wetlands, and lot lines - frequently fix the house location before any architect is hired. Land contracts on unsewered parcels are routinely written contingent on these tests, for good reason.

Investigation Three: The Water - Grading and Drainage

Nearly every chronic below-roof problem a house develops is a water problem, and nearly all of it traces to grading. The governing rules are few and firm: ground falls away from the building (six inches in the first ten feet), every flow has a legal destination, roof water is piped well clear of the foundation, structural fill is compacted in tested lifts, and exposed soil is stabilized before it erodes. Swales, berms, French drains, footing drains, and dry wells are the vocabulary; the design that deploys them is drawn together with house placement, not after it.

Investigation Four: The Rules - Zoning and the Buildable Envelope

The final investigation is drawn in the bylaw rather than the ground. Setbacks, lot coverage, height limits, and frontage minimums define a box; easements, wetland buffers, and overlay districts shrink it further; and what remains - the buildable envelope - is what the lot truly offers. Mapping it is arithmetic anyone can do with the boundary survey and the dimensional table, and doing it before committing reveals which parcels hold the intended house as-of-right and which are premised on winning a variance, which is to say premised on risk.

The investigations interlock: the survey feeds the drainage design, the soils fix the septic geometry, and the zoning envelope arbitrates among them. Sequencing them into a single coordinated site plan - with utilities, access, and the house program resolved together - is what turns four separate reports into one buildable property.

What should I investigate before buying land?

All four layers, proportionate to the price at stake: a topographic reconnaissance, perc contingency where sewer is absent, a drainage walk after hard rain, and the zoning envelope drawn on the boundary survey. Days of diligence routinely reprice a parcel by more than the diligence costs.

How much does site work cost relative to the house?

Commonly ten to twenty-five percent of total project cost, with steep, ledgy, or wet sites running higher. It is the most site-variable line in any budget - which is exactly why the investigations precede the offer.

What makes a lot effectively unbuildable?

Usually a stack of constraints rather than one: failed perc across the usable area, wetland buffers overlapping the zoning envelope, or grades that make access uneconomical. Each alone may have an engineered answer; together they can consume the budget the house needed.

When do I need a civil engineer versus a surveyor?

The surveyor documents what exists - boundaries, terrain, features. The civil engineer designs what will change - grading, drainage, septic, stormwater compliance. Most custom projects need both, in that order, often from collaborating firms.

Can a difficult lot be a good buy?

Frequently - difficulty is priced in, and some "problems" are assets in disguise: a cross-slope that yields a walk-out lower level, or ledge that becomes landscape. The distinction between a bargain and a trap is whether the difficulties were measured before the purchase or discovered after it.

What is the most common site planning mistake?

Designing the house first and the site second. A floor plan conceived on flat paper then forced onto real ground pays for the mismatch in retaining walls, fill, and redesign - the ground votes first whether or not it is consulted.

How long does site preparation take before construction can start?

Investigations and design commonly run two to six months - surveys, perc testing in its seasonal window, engineering, and permitting each have their own clocks, and wetlands or variance filings add public-hearing timelines. The physical work of clearing, grading, and utilities is faster than the paperwork that authorizes it, which is why experienced schedules start the regulatory clock at purchase.

What utilities questions should be answered before design?

Where power, water, gas, and communications will enter, from how far, and at whose cost. Long private drives make utility trenching a real budget line; off-grid distances change the conversation entirely; and well drilling, where municipal water is absent, joins the perc test as a due-diligence item since well and septic components must keep regulated distances from each other on the same finite lot.
