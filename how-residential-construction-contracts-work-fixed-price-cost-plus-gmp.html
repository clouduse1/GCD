How Residential Construction Contracts Work: Fixed-Price, Cost-Plus, GMP

By the Editorial Team | Published: July 2026 | Last Updated: July 2026

The Document That Decides Who Carries the Risk

A construction contract is fundamentally a risk-allocation device. Materials fluctuate in price, weather interrupts schedules, and hidden conditions emerge from the ground - someone has to absorb those uncertainties, and the contract structure decides who. Homeowners often focus on the bottom-line number while overlooking the pricing model behind it, yet the model matters more than the number.

Three pricing structures dominate residential construction: fixed-price, cost-plus, and cost-plus with a guaranteed maximum price. Each one shifts risk differently between owner and builder, and each rewards a different kind of project.

Fixed-Price (Lump Sum) Contracts

In a fixed-price contract, the builder commits to delivering a defined scope of work for a single stated sum. If materials cost more than estimated or labor runs long, the builder absorbs the overrun; if the builder performs efficiently, the savings become margin. The owner's exposure is limited to the contract amount plus any changes the owner initiates.

The strength of fixed-price is certainty - but that certainty is only as good as the drawings behind it. A fixed price against incomplete documents is an illusion: every gap in the drawings becomes a change order priced without competition. Fixed-price works best when design is fully developed and the owner intends to make few changes.

Builders necessarily include contingency in a lump sum to cover the risk they are accepting. Owners pay for that certainty whether or not the risks materialize.

Cost-Plus Contracts

A cost-plus contract reimburses the builder for actual costs - labor, materials, subcontracts, equipment - plus an agreed fee, expressed either as a percentage of cost or a fixed amount. The owner sees the real invoices, and pricing transparency is the model's defining virtue.

The corresponding weakness is open-ended exposure: the owner carries cost risk, and with a percentage fee, the builder's compensation grows as costs grow. Well-drafted cost-plus agreements counter this with a fixed fee rather than a percentage, defined billing categories, documented labor rates, and regular open-book accounting.

Cost-plus suits projects where scope is genuinely uncertain - renovations of older structures, projects begun before design is finalized, or owners who plan to make decisions along the way and want to pay actual cost rather than padded estimates for that flexibility.

Guaranteed Maximum Price (GMP)

A GMP contract is cost-plus with a ceiling. The builder is reimbursed for actual costs plus a fee, but total billing cannot exceed the guaranteed maximum; overruns beyond the cap are the builder's problem. Savings below the cap either return to the owner or are shared under an agreed split, which gives the builder an incentive to control cost.

GMP occupies the middle ground: the transparency of cost-plus with a defined worst case. Its complexity lies in administration - the contract must define precisely what counts as reimbursable cost, how contingency is spent and reported, and how savings are calculated at closeout.

Contingency: The Honest Line Item

Every construction budget contains uncertainty; the only question is whether it is visible. A contingency is a named reserve - commonly five to ten percent on new construction, more on renovation - set aside for conditions that cannot be known in advance. In fixed-price contracts the contingency exists but is hidden inside the builder's number; in cost-plus and GMP contracts it appears as a line the owner can see, with the contract defining who authorizes spending from it and who keeps what remains.

Owners sometimes read a visible contingency as pessimism and push to delete it. This is backwards: the contingency is what prevents mid-project financing crises, and projects that strike it do not eliminate the uncertainty - they merely convert it into disputes. A well-run project reports contingency status at every billing cycle, so the reserve drains transparently rather than surprising anyone at the end.

Clauses That Matter in Every Model

Allowances are placeholder amounts for unselected items; they should be realistic, clearly listed, and reconciled against actual selections. Change orders should require written scope, price, and schedule impact approved before the work proceeds. Payment schedules should follow completed milestones, and retainage - a small percentage withheld from each payment until completion - protects the owner's leverage through closeout.

Escalation clauses, which pass certain material price increases to the owner, became common after the lumber volatility of recent years. They are not inherently unfair, but they should name the specific materials covered, the index used to measure increases, and a threshold below which the builder absorbs the movement.

The pricing model also interacts with how the project is delivered. Integrated firms frequently pair GMP or fixed-price structures with continuous estimating during design, a relationship worth understanding before selecting any design-build firm.

Which contract type is cheapest?

None is inherently cheapest. Fixed-price embeds a risk premium; cost-plus exposes the owner to overruns; GMP adds administrative cost. The cheapest contract is the one matched to how complete the design is and how much the owner intends to change it.

What is a fair fee on a cost-plus contract?

Residential builder fees commonly fall in the range of ten to twenty percent of cost depending on region, project complexity, and what overhead the fee must cover. More important than the percentage is defining which costs are reimbursable and which are covered by the fee.

What is retainage?

Retainage is a percentage - often five to ten percent - withheld from each progress payment and released at substantial completion or after punch-list work. It keeps both parties motivated to finish the last details.

Can a fixed-price contract still go over budget?

Yes, through owner-initiated changes, unrealistic allowances, and concealed-condition clauses covering things no one could see, such as ledge or buried debris. The fixed price covers the defined scope, not everything that could possibly happen.

Should a lawyer review the contract?

For a project of any significant value, yes. A construction attorney's review costs a fraction of one change-order dispute and typically improves payment, warranty, and termination terms the owner would not think to negotiate.

What is a time-and-materials arrangement, and when is it appropriate?

Time-and-materials is cost-plus at its simplest: billed labor hours at stated rates plus materials, usually with a markup. It suits small, genuinely unpredictable work - exploratory demolition, repairs of unknown extent - where estimating would be fiction. For substantial projects it provides too little cost discipline, and most owners should insist on one of the three structured models instead.

Can the contract type be changed mid-project?

Yes, by mutual agreement, and one conversion is common by design: progressive arrangements that begin cost-plus during early phases and convert to a fixed price or GMP once the design and selections are firm enough to price responsibly. The conversion terms - what is known, what remains allowanced, how contingency carries over - should be written into the original agreement rather than improvised later.
